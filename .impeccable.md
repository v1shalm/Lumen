## Design Context

### Users
Solo researchers and knowledge workers doing deep, focused analysis — competitive intelligence, market research, academic synthesis. They work in long sessions, deal with high cognitive load, and need the tool to *get out of their way*. They are technically literate but not necessarily developers. They reach for Lumen when they need to synthesize 20+ scattered sources into a single defensible analysis. They do not tolerate clutter.

### Brand Personality
**Sharp, cerebral, assured.**

Lumen is the tool a serious researcher would trust. It does not shout. It does not glow. It presents information with the quiet confidence of a good editorial layout — the kind that makes you feel like the material itself is doing the work. Voice: precise, direct, never playful. Tone: collegial expert, not assistant.

Emotional goal: *quiet intellectual confidence* — like sitting at a well-organized research desk where everything is exactly where you expect it.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Editorial-minimal with technical rigor.**

References:
- **hex.inc** — asymmetric editorial grid, opinionated use of negative space, type-first hierarchy, flush-left brutalist confidence
- **ElevenLabs** — clean dark surfaces, precise typography, dense-but-legible product UI, technical without being cold
- **Firecrawl** — developer-grade minimalism, no decorative chrome, every element earns its place, monospace as punctuation not personality

Anti-references (explicitly avoid):
- Generic SaaS dashboards (card grids, icon-above-heading templates, hero metrics)
- Notion-style bland neutral UI — too passive, no point of view
- AI aesthetic clichés: purple-to-blue gradients, cyan glows, glassmorphism, gradient text, neon on dark

**Theme: Light mode primary.** Researchers work in daylight focus sessions. The warmth of the orange accent reads better on light surfaces and feels more editorial than electric.

**Color:** Retain the warm orange primary (`oklch(0.648 0.2 35.49)`) as the signature accent. Use it sparingly — status indicators, active states, key CTAs. Neutrals should be tinted slightly warm (toward the orange hue), never pure gray. No blue. No purple-cyan.

**Typography:** Choose fonts that feel like a well-produced research publication — not a startup landing page. Avoid all fonts in the Impeccable reflex-reject list (especially Inter, which is currently in use and must be replaced). Pair a distinctive text face (something with personality at small sizes) with a contrasting display or mono element for labels and metadata.

### Design Principles
1. **Restraint is the feature.** Every element competes for a researcher's attention. If it doesn't serve the task, remove it.
2. **Typography carries the hierarchy.** Size, weight, and spacing do the work — not color, not icons, not cards.
3. **The interface defers to the content.** Chrome fades; the synthesized knowledge leads. UI elements should feel like good furniture, not decoration.
4. **Orange is rare and therefore powerful.** Use the accent color with discipline — it marks what matters, not what's pretty.
5. **Density with breathing room.** Information-dense layouts (like ElevenLabs or Firecrawl) but with editorial-grade spacing between sections. Tight within groups, generous between them.
